Getting a Spanish Visa is way simpler and quicker than getting a French one. Spanish bureaucracy is more disorganized that French bureaucracy, so it is in your interest to make things easier for them.

A lot of bureaucratic information about Spain is only available in Spanish. Try to get a native Spanish speaker to help you if you get confused.

Where to apply?

You have to apply in the country where you are a resident. Since I was a resident of France, I had to apply there, even though my nationality was not French.

Note that one applies for visas at consulates, not embassies, though it is common for embassies to have a consulate in the same building.

To know exactly where to apply for the Visa, I had to call and write emails to the Spanish consulates close to Nancy. There is one in Strassbourg and one in Paris. Even though the consulate in Strassbourg is the closest one to Nancy, it is the consulate in Paris that handles applications to student visas.

Necessary documents

In the same email telling me where to apply, the consulate sent me the list of documents for the application. I had to send the original documents via physical mail, except for my passport and residence permit (copies are enough).

I used the letter for the Erasmus Mundus scholarship.

Criminal record

You have to send them your criminal records from all the countries where you have been a resident in the last five years, and they may have to be apostilled. This can get kind of complicated.

In my case, I was a resident of France at the moment, and had been in my home country before that.

  • French criminal record:

    • In French it is called extrait de casier judiciare
    • You can request it in this website.
    • It is very easy to request it if you have a France Connect account.
    • You can request it for free in digital form or physical form.
    • The physical form takes two weeks to arrive.
    • Since this was a document from the French government and the Spanish consulate was in France, it did not have to be apostilled.

  • Criminal record from my home country

    • I had to call and write emails to the closest consulate of my home country and figure out the process.
    • I had to pay around 60 € to my consulate, and send some documents via physical mail.
    • It took almost two months to get the criminal record.
    • They sent it to me with an official seal equivalent to an apostille.

Health certificate

To get a Spanish visa you need a health certificate saying that you do not have any contagious sickness.

Getting this document is very confusing, because apparently Spain is the only country that requires it. For this reason, it is not clear where to ask for it, who should sign it, how much it should cost, etc.

After talking with other LCT students about their experiences, it looks like the process to get this document changes depending on each country. Ask your local Spanish consulate, but don't expect clear instructions.

In my case, I went to my médecin traitant (general practitioner doctor in France) and showed the requirements from the consulate. The doctor wrote the following letter (but in French) for free:

"""
<Doctor name>
<Doctor address address>
<My City>

CERTIFICATE OF NON-CONTAGIOUS DISEASES

I, <Dr Name>, medical doctor, certify that today I have examined Mr. <my Name>, 
born on the <date of birth>, and he does not present any clinical symptom 
of contagious diseases, chronic or acute.

<Signature and Seal>
<City and Date>

""";

Accomodation

To apply for the visa you have to provide your address in Spain. Luckily there is a workaround for this, if you have not yet looked for accomodation.

You can ask the LCT coordinator for a letter about the accomodation service of the University of the Basque country.

  • Note that the UBC does not have student residences, so you will have to look for accomodation in the private sector.
  • The letter basically says that the UBC will help you find accomodation.
    • In reality, they won't help you, but this letter is enough to satisfy the Spanish bureaucracy for the Visa.

Application form

In the same email telling me where to apply, the consulate sent me a form to fill, which asks for:

  • Basic personal information

    • Full name
    • date of birth
    • nationality
    • national ID number
    • residence permit number (if any)

  • Current profession (Student)

  • Estimated date of arrival
  • Reason for travelling (studies)
  • Number of entries to Spain (multiple)
  • Address in Spain (refer to the section above about accomodation)
    • I just wrote "please read the attached document about accomodation"
  • Information from the hosting institution
    • Name (University of the Basque country)
    • Address
    • Phone number (ask the LCT coordinator)
    • email (from the LCT coordinator)
    • estimated dates of beginning and end of studies

There are many other sections in the form, like sections about employment or refugee status, but you don't have to fill them, because they don't apply to students.

Getting a response

One month and a half after sending the documents for the Visa application to the Spanish consulate in Paris, I got an email saying my Visa had been approved!

Additionally, since I was part of the Erasmus Mundus program, I did not have to pay for the Visa.

Picking up the Spanish Visa

I had to book an appointment to pick up the Visa in person.

For the Spanish consulate in Paris, it is more convenient to book an appointment in the morning, because it takes a couple of hours to print the Visa after leaving your passport there. If you book an appointment for late in the afternoon, it is very likely that you will have to come back the next day. The process may be different at other consulates.
